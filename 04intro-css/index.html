Exposure

Exposure is the amount of light that reaches your camera sensor. There are three components that go into exposure: ISO, Shutter Speed, and Aperture (f/stop).

ISO

ISO is the level of sensitivity your camera has to light. ISO aslo contributes to the amount of grain or noise your photo has. The lower the ISO the less sensitive it is to light and the less grain/noise you will have. The higher the ISO, the more sensitive it is to the light and the more grain/noise you will have.

  1. ISO 100: Full sun and no shade
  2. ISO 200: Shade, overcast day, or inside near a window
  3. ISO 400: Deep shade or heavily overcast day
  4. ISO 640-800: Early or late hours of the day; sunrise or sunset
  5. ISO 800: Bright interiors
  6. ISO 1000: Mid-level lighting condition, indoors or outdoors
  7. ISO 1250: Low-light interiors or post-sunset
  8. ISO 1600: Extremely low light

Shutter Speed

Shutter Speed is the length of time that the camera shutter is open to expose light into the camera sensor.

1/4000 - 1/1000:
Freezing fast moving objects
1/250 - 1/60:
Everyday photos, objects are still or barely moving
1/30 - 10":
Capturing motion with blur

Aperature (f-stop)

Aperature is the size of the opening of your camera lens. This controls the amount of light that reaches the sensor as well as the depth of field. The wider the opening the more light it has as well as a shallow depth of field. The smaller the opening the darker the photo will be as well as a deep depth of field.

  • f/1.4: Bright and blurred background
  • f/22: Darker and everything in focus

Exposure Triangle

Proper exposure is acheived by having the ISO, aperature and shutter speed coming into balance. When one point of the triangle is moved in one direction, another point of the triangle must move the same distance in the opposite direction to maintain exposure.
